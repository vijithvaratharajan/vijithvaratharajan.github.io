# The Problem with Purpose-Built Games in SLA Research

*29 June 2026*

Most of what we know about digital game-based language learning comes from games that were designed to teach. Language learning apps, purpose-built vocabulary trainers, gamified platforms with progress bars and XP systems. The research on these tools is substantial, reasonably well-controlled, and consistently positive in its conclusions. And it tells us very little about what actually happens when a learner sits down with a commercial game for twelve hours.

This gap is not a footnote. It is arguably the central methodological problem of the field.

## Why purpose-built games are the wrong test case

When a researcher evaluates a game designed explicitly to teach language, they are measuring a system whose primary function is instruction. The vocabulary is selected, the repetition is deliberate, the feedback loops are engineered for acquisition. The ecological validity of these findings is, at best, thin. Most learners who encounter English through games are not using tools designed for them. They are playing Disco Elysium or The Witcher 3 or Red Dead Redemption 2 — titles whose developers had no particular interest in language pedagogy and built entire textual worlds anyway.

The distinction matters because what makes commercial games interesting as learning environments is precisely that they were not optimised for teaching. Language in these games is situated within consequential narrative contexts. A dialogue choice in Disco Elysium does not exist to drill vocabulary; it exists to reveal character, alter relationships, and change the world. The word "laconic" appears in Disco Elysium because it is the right word, not because a curriculum designer placed it at the B2 level. That is a fundamentally different exposure condition from a vocabulary app surfacing the same word in a spaced repetition queue.

Situated learning theory (Lave and Wenger, 1991) gives us language for this distinction. Knowledge acquired in a situated environment — where it is functionally embedded in activity, consequence, and social meaning — generalises differently than knowledge acquired in decontextualised repetition. Whether that theoretical commitment holds under experimental conditions for vocabulary specifically acquired through narrative gameplay is an open empirical question. But it is not a question that studies of purpose-built educational games can answer.

## What the extramural evidence suggests

The most important work in this space has come not from experimental studies but from correlational and naturalistic research. Sundqvist and Sylven's longitudinal work on Swedish learners of English established that out-of-school game engagement was associated with vocabulary gains independent of formal instruction, and that this effect was driven by voluntary engagement with commercial titles rather than educational software. The mechanism they identified — extended, motivated exposure to authentic language in meaningful contexts — aligns precisely with what a motivated learner experiences playing a narrative RPG for sixty hours.

What that body of work does not give us is mechanistic detail. We know the association exists. We do not know, with any precision, which features of commercial game environments drive lexical acquisition, which learner dispositions moderate the effect, or whether gains persist after the engagement period ends. The short timescales of most experimental DGBLL studies make the retention question almost entirely unaddressed.

## What this means for research design

None of this is an argument against studying educational games. It is an argument that the field has systematically under-invested in studying the environments where the most consequential informal language acquisition actually occurs. A learner who has spent two hundred hours with The Witcher 3 in English has been exposed to a volume and variety of language that no purpose-built educational game can replicate. Whether that exposure translates into measurable, transferable competence — and under what conditions it does — is the question that matters for educators, curriculum developers, and anyone interested in the ecology of language acquisition beyond the classroom.

Commercial games are inconvenient research objects. They are large, uncontrolled, and resist the clean operationalisation that experimental designs require. But the research questions they raise are more ecologically valid, more generalisable, and ultimately more useful than the questions that purpose-built games answer very tidily.
